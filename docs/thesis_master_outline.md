# Thesis Outline

## Motivation

Post-COVID ECB policy raises a macro-financial transmission question: did easing shocks transmit most clearly through housing-credit and financial-intermediation channels, or did they pass through with comparable strength into compensation-linked real-economy variables?

## Research Question

Did post-COVID ECB monetary easing transmit more strongly through housing-credit and financial-intermediation channels than through compensation-linked real-economy variables?

## Identification

The shock is a high-frequency ECB announcement surprise. Monthly aggregation keeps the timing advantage while allowing comparison with monthly macro-financial variables. Information effects remain a central interpretation boundary.

## Data

The analysis uses house-purchase lending growth, pure-new mortgage flows, lending spreads, credit quantities, bank/intermediation variables, financial-market responses, and compensation-linked indicators. Lower-frequency house-price and compensation series are not interpolated.

## Econometrics

Monthly impulse responses compare channel dynamics at short, medium, and longer horizons. Cumulative responses are used for persistence comparisons.

## Results

Housing-credit responses are the clearest and most durable. Lending spreads and intermediation variables support the mechanism. Financial-market responses are mixed, especially because the DAX response is negative and information-sensitive. Compensation-linked responses are weaker and less consistent.

## Robustness

Alternative shocks, clean events, rolling estimates, recursive estimates, exclusions, uncertainty checks, and alternative proxies preserve the main hierarchy most clearly for house-purchase lending growth and lending spreads.

## Boundary

The thesis identifies reduced-form transmission to policy-news shocks. It does not identify welfare, inequality, redistribution, exact house-price effects, exact compensation effects, or structural bank mediation.
